A Case Study: The CKD-EPI Equation and Cisplatin Therapy

Consider a Urologist evaluating the impact of adopting the race-free CKD-EPI 2021 equation to determine eligibility for cisplatin therapy among bladder cancer patients (Figure 1 above). For over a decade, the standard equation has been the CKD-EPI 2009, which requires the following inputs:

Definition of CKD-EPI The CKD-EPI (Chronic Kidney Disease Epidemiology Collaboration) equation is a formula often used to estimate the Glomerular Filtration Rate (GFR), a measure of kidney function. The CKD-EPI 2009 equation, while more accurate than the older MDRD (Modification of Diet in Renal Disease) equation, raised issues of justice and equity in the United States due to its inclusion of race as a variable. It's worth noting that these issues have yet to garner attention in other countries.

  1. Serum Creatinine (SCr): A byproduct of muscle metabolism, measured in mg/dL or ¬µmol/L.

  2. Age: The patient‚Äôs age in years.

  3. Sex: Biological sex (Male or Female), relevant due to differences in muscle mass and consequently, creatinine levels.

  4. Race: Originally included as a variable in the CKD-EPI 2009 equation, race has since become a point of ongoing debate due to ethical concerns.

The Original Equations

The CKD-EPI 2009 equation has specific formulations for subgroups defined by these variables, which are then used to calculate the Glomerular Filtration Rate (GFR).

  • For females with SCr ‚â§ 0.7 mg/dL: \(GFR = 144 \times \left( \frac{{SCr}}{0.7} \right)^{-0.329} \times 0.993^{Age}\)

  • For females with SCr > 0.7 mg/dL: \(GFR = 144 \times \left( \frac{{SCr}}{0.7} \right)^{-1.209} \times 0.993^{Age}\)

  • For males with SCr ‚â§ 0.9 mg/dL: \(GFR = 141 \times \left( \frac{{SCr}}{0.9} \right)^{-0.411} \times 0.993^{Age}\)

  • For males with SCr > 0.9 mg/dL: \(GFR = 141 \times \left( \frac{{SCr}}{0.9} \right)^{-1.209} \times 0.993^{Age}\)

  • For African Americans, the estimated GFR is then multiplied by 1.159.

The Impact of a Race-Free Equation

By shifting to the new race-free equation, a variety of questions arise:

  • How does this change affect the classification of bladder cancer patients?

  • What is the new proportion of patients who can access cisplatin?

  • What are the anticipated changes in adverse effects?

  • Are there patients who may now be inappropriately classified as eligible for cisplatin therapy?

Such inquiries require rigorous empirical investigation. How would you approach this question from a study design perspective?
